In a kitchen, your knife roll is your livelihood. In a mining camp, bad gear gets you killed, or at the very least, fired. Software is safer, but the principle holds. Tools are leverage. If your environment is constantly fighting you, you are bleeding time and focus. 

Here is what I use to read, write, and build. No cloud dependencies, no marketing bullshit. Just local-first tools that do their job, respect the hardware, and get out of the way.

## I. The Iron & The OS
{: #the-iron}

I run a **ThinkPad T14s AMD Gen 6** equipped with 32GB of RAM. It is a workhorse, not a lifestyle accessory. The AMD architecture respects the laws of thermodynamics; compiling code shouldn't turn your laptop into a space heater. The operating system is **Fedora 44 Workstation** under GNOME and Wayland. Wayland is finally stable, and Fedora provides an aggressive but reliable foundation that doesn't treat you like a perpetual beta tester. I keep Windows 11 Pro on a dual-boot because reality occasionally demands it, but the actual work happens in Linux. 

My phone is an **iPhone 16 Pro**. I value good typography, polished hardware, and an opinionated ecosystem when I'm away from the keyboard, even if I strictly reject that locked-down philosophy at my desk. I'm strongly considering a jump to the Pixel, but I've been an iPhone user my entire life.

## II. Aesthetics as Ergonomics
{: #aesthetics}

Visuals aren't vanity; they dictate how long you can stare at a screen before your eyes bleed. I favor a minimalist, industrial look. The terminal runs **Gruber Dark**. Everything else—from Neovim to standalone GUI tools—is bathed in [**Kanagawa Dragon**](https://github.com/rebelot/kanagawa.nvim). Dark modes aren't just an aesthetic preference; they are defensive architecture against ocular fatigue during twelve-hour stretches.

I do not tolerate system default fonts. My terminal and code are rendered in **TX-02** (semi-light, 18pt). Prose in Emacs gets **Tiempos Text**. These are premium, licensed fonts. Good typography is a cheap luxury that pays compounding dividends on your attention span. TX-02 provides aggressive, unambiguous glyph separation—a zero and an 'O' should never look like siblings when you're auditing low-level systems logic.

## III. The Two-Brain Architecture
{: #two-brain}

Trying to make one piece of software do everything is a fool's errand. It guarantees mediocrity. VSCode wants to be an operating system; I want an editor. I enforce a strict separation of concerns between writing code and running my life. 

* **The Brain ([Doom Emacs](https://github.com/doomemacs/doomemacs)):** This is the operational backend for my life. It handles my GTD workflow, spaced repetition, journaling, course management, and tracking my studies at Northern College. Why Emacs? Because **Org-mode** is structurally invincible. It is the most robust plaintext data format ever created. Decades from now, long after proprietary SaaS apps are dead and their servers are shut down, my `config.org` and plaintext files will still be perfectly readable.
* **The Scalpel (LazyVim):** This is the code editor. Emacs is a Lisp machine, which makes it heavy. I don't write code there. [LazyVim](https://www.lazyvim.org/) strips the environment down to the absolute minimum required to pass keystrokes to an LSP (`rust-analyzer`, `pyright`, `jdtls`, and `clangd` for C and C++). It has a transparent background, ruthless minimalism, and zero bloat. You go in, cut the code, and get out.

## IV. The Terminal Ecosystem
{: #the-terminal}

Nostalgia for 1980s POSIX defaults is a disease. I appreciate the minimalism of the Suckless ecosystem and have patched my share of `dwm` and `st` in the past, but I don't romanticize archaic GNU coreutils when modern, multithreaded alternatives exist. My shell environment is built entirely on modern, high-performance replacements, mostly written in Rust or Go.

**Ghostty** ([ghostty.org](https://ghostty.org/)) is the terminal emulator, running **zsh**. It is GPU-accelerated and handles complex font ligatures without stuttering. 

I use `eza` ([github.com/eza-community/eza](https://github.com/eza-community/eza)) instead of `ls`, `bat` ([github.com/sharkdp/bat](https://github.com/sharkdp/bat)) instead of `cat`, `rg` ([github.com/BurntSushi/ripgrep](https://github.com/BurntSushi/ripgrep)) instead of `grep`, and `fd` instead of `find`. Why? Because `rg` respects my `.gitignore` by default and executes searches orders of magnitude faster. `bat` gives me syntax highlighting instantly. `zoxide` learns my directory habits so I don't have to type out absolute paths. `lazygit` handles version control, with `delta` wired in as the git pager. The tooling does the heavy lifting so I can focus on the output.

## V. The Desktop & Application Stack
{: #desktop-stack}

<img width="1920" height="1200" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/16c93e57-c00e-4db8-b7b0-d731ba872dcb" />

GNOME needs discipline to be useful out of the box. I use **Pop Shell** ([extensions.gnome.org](https://extensions.gnome.org/extension/4000/pop-shell/)) to enforce window tiling, **Vitals** to keep an eye on system metrics, and **Blur My Shell** for a cohesive aesthetic.

My GUI toolchain is a defensive architecture. It actively rejects the decay of modern computing—Electron-wrapped web apps and telemetry-harvesting SaaS subscriptions.

* **Web:** **Zen Browser** ([zen-browser.app](https://zen-browser.app/)). It offers the privacy and engine of Firefox without the Mozilla-mandated bloatware.
* **Reading & Media:** **Calibre** ([calibre-ebook.com](https://calibre-ebook.com/)) holds my curated archive of over 4,200 ebooks. **Zotero** ([zotero.org](https://zotero.org/)) organizes academic references. Neither phones home. You own the library, not a corporation. I also use **YACReader** ([yacreader.com](https://www.yacreader.com/)) to parse through hundreds of Tabletop RPG PDFs, **Kasts** for podcasts, and **Nicotine+** ([nicotine-plus.org](https://nicotine-plus.org/)) for hunting down 90s East Coast rap and Industrial Techno on Soulseek.
* **Comms:** **Halloy** ([github.com/squidowl/halloy](https://github.com/squidowl/halloy)) for IRC, **Fractal** for Matrix, **Tuba** for Mastodon. Native applications, zero browser tabs required.
* **Finance:** **Actual** ([actualbudget.org](https://actualbudget.org/)) and **hledger** ([hledger.org](https://hledger.org/)) for budgeting. Financial data is sensitive. Renting a cloud service to view your own money is insane. Double-entry plaintext accounting forces mathematical discipline, and Actual provides a local-first UI without the rent-seeking subscription.
* **Security:** **1Password**. I am militant about local-first software, but cryptography is hard. Rolling your own sync protocol for credentials is a fool's errand. You outsource this to audited professionals.

For RSS, I use an **Inoreader** backend. Right now, I'm reading feeds on NewsFlash, but I'm actively migrating to my own native reader.

## VI. Custom Code: Building What I Need
{: #custom-code}

Eventually, you get tired of compromising and just build the tool yourself. General-purpose software inevitably degrades. By building native Linux GTK4 apps backed by SQLite in WAL mode, I guarantee performance. No network latency, no web-view overhead.

* **Framework:** I wrote this because I wanted a document viewer that didn't drag like an anchor. It opens immediately and gets out of the way.
* **Viaduct:** My own NetNewsWire port for Linux. I am slowly moving my RSS reading entirely here.
* **Quarry & Lattice:** Heavy-duty archivism requires dedicated tools. Quarry mass-polishes metadata for Calibre, and Lattice manages a local 6,000-track music library with extreme prejudice.
* **Atrium:** Task management is currently split—Org-mode for school, Todoist with Rin for household chores. But I am slowly eating both workflows with Atrium. It's an Org-mode app wearing an OmniFocus and Things 3 disguise. I get the aesthetic calm of a modern GUI with the architectural permanence of a SQLite database that round-trips to plaintext, so I don't have to launch Emacs just to see what I'm doing today.

## VII. The iPhone: Triage & Consumption
{: #iphone}

A phone is a consumption and triage device. If you are doing real work on a 6-inch glass rectangle, something has gone wrong in your life. The homescreen is arranged for speed, not scrolling. I dictate where the attention goes.

**Page One:**
Anchored at the top is a large **Fantastical** widget. It synthesizes calendars better than Apple’s native app and parses natural language input instantly. The dock is the core, holding a **Learning** folder (Blackboard, Reminders, PCalc, Soulver), Messages, Safari, and **Gemini**. I maintain subscriptions to both ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro, keeping Claude on the primary grid for rapid querying.

The rest of the grid:
* **Standalone Apps:** **Raindrop.io** for bookmarking, **Overcast** because it respects audio engineering, **Claude**, **foobar2000**, and **Awful** (for the SomethingAwful forums, because some habits refuse to die).
* **Important Folder:** Tangerine, Wealthsimple, Passwords, 1Password, Outlook, Anylist, Authy, LocalSend, Cronometer.
* **Feeds Folder:** Reddit, WhatsApp, Mail, **Ice Cubes** (the only Mastodon client with acceptable UX), HEY email, **NetNewsWire** (syncing with Inoreader so I don't lose my place away from the desk).
* **Social Folder:** Discord, LinkedIn, Steam, Spotify, Snapchat, Phone, LibraryThing, GitHub.

**Page Two:**
Pure utility. A large **Todoist** widget showing Today's tasks ensures shared execution doesn't slip through the cracks, with a smart rotating widget underneath it taking up the rest of the screen (CARROT Weather, Mail, Gmail).
