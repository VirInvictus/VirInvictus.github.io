In a kitchen, your knife roll is your livelihood. In a mining camp, bad gear gets you killed, or at the very least, fired. Software is safer, but the principle holds. Tools are leverage. If your environment is constantly fighting you, you are bleeding time and focus. 

Here is what I use to read, write, and build. No cloud dependencies, no marketing bullshit. Just local-first tools that do their job, respect the hardware, and get out of the way.

## I. The Iron & The OS
{: #the-iron}

I run a **ThinkPad T14s AMD Gen 6** equipped with 32GB of RAM. It is a workhorse, not a lifestyle accessory, vastly superior to thermal-throttling Intel MacBooks or flimsy ultrabooks. The AMD architecture respects the laws of thermodynamics; compiling code shouldn't turn your laptop into a space heater. The operating system is **Fedora 44 Workstation** under GNOME and Wayland. Wayland is finally stable, and unlike Arch, which demands constant babysitting, or Ubuntu, which is crippled by corporate bloat, Fedora provides an aggressive but reliable foundation that doesn't treat you like a perpetual beta tester. I keep Windows 11 Pro on a dual-boot because reality occasionally demands it, but the actual work happens in Linux. 

My phone is an **iPhone 16 Pro**. I value good typography, polished hardware, and an opinionated ecosystem when I'm away from the keyboard, even if I strictly reject that locked-down philosophy at my desk. Android flagships are fine, but iOS simply outclasses them in long-term reliability, standby battery life, and core app quality. I'm strongly considering a jump to the Pixel, but I've been an iPhone user my entire life.

## II. Aesthetics as Ergonomics
{: #aesthetics}

Visuals aren't vanity; they dictate how long you can stare at a screen before your eyes bleed. I favor a minimalist, industrial look. The terminal runs **Gruber Dark**. Everything else—from Neovim to standalone GUI tools—is bathed in [**Kanagawa Dragon**](https://github.com/rebelot/kanagawa.nvim). Dark modes aren't just an aesthetic preference; they are defensive architecture against ocular fatigue during twelve-hour stretches. Kanagawa Dragon is simply better than generic dark modes like Dracula or Nord because it perfectly balances contrast without the searing neon highlights that fatigue the retina.

I do not tolerate system default fonts. My terminal and code are rendered in **TX-02** (semi-light, 18pt). Prose in Emacs gets **Tiempos Text**. These are premium, licensed fonts that obliterate free alternatives like Fira Code or standard system serifs in readability. Good typography is a cheap luxury that pays compounding dividends on your attention span. TX-02 provides aggressive, unambiguous glyph separation—a zero and an 'O' should never look like siblings when you're auditing low-level systems logic.

## III. The Two-Brain Architecture
{: #two-brain}

Trying to make one piece of software do everything is a fool's errand. It guarantees mediocrity. VSCode wants to be an operating system; I want an editor. I enforce a strict separation of concerns between writing code and running my life. 

* **The Brain ([Doom Emacs](https://github.com/doomemacs/doomemacs)):** This is the operational backend for my life. It handles my GTD workflow, spaced repetition, journaling, course management, and tracking my studies at Northern College. Why Emacs? Because **Org-mode** is structurally invincible. Unlike Notion, Obsidian, or Roam—which lock your thoughts in proprietary databases or slow Electron wrappers—Org-mode is the most robust plaintext data format ever created. Decades from now, long after proprietary SaaS apps are dead and their servers are shut down, my `config.org` and plaintext files will still be perfectly readable.
* **The Scalpel ([LazyVim](https://www.lazyvim.org/)):** This is the code editor. Emacs is a Lisp machine, which makes it heavy. I don't write code there. LazyVim strips the environment down to the absolute minimum required to pass keystrokes to an LSP (`rust-analyzer`, `pyright`, `jdtls`, and `clangd` for C and C++). Unlike VSCode or JetBrains IDEs that consume gigabytes of RAM just to blink a cursor, it has a transparent background, ruthless minimalism, and zero bloat. You go in, cut the code, and get out.

## IV. The Terminal Ecosystem
{: #the-terminal}

Nostalgia for 1980s POSIX defaults is a disease. I appreciate the minimalism of the Suckless ecosystem and have patched my share of `dwm` and `st` in the past, but I don't romanticize archaic GNU coreutils when modern, multithreaded alternatives exist. My shell environment is built entirely on modern, high-performance replacements, mostly written in Rust or Go.

**Ghostty** ([ghostty.org](https://ghostty.org/)) is the terminal emulator, running **zsh**. It outperforms Alacritty and Kitty in raw rendering speed, is entirely GPU-accelerated, and handles complex font ligatures without the stuttering found in older emulators. 

I use `eza` ([github.com/eza-community/eza](https://github.com/eza-community/eza)) instead of `ls`, `bat` ([github.com/sharkdp/bat](https://github.com/sharkdp/bat)) instead of `cat`, `rg` ([github.com/BurntSushi/ripgrep](https://github.com/BurntSushi/ripgrep)) instead of `grep`, and `fd` instead of `find`. Why? Because `rg` destroys GNU `grep` in performance benchmarks and respects my `.gitignore` by default. `bat` beats `cat` by giving me instant syntax highlighting. `zoxide` runs circles around standard `cd` by learning my directory habits, and `lazygit` is simply the fastest, most intuitive terminal UI for git in existence, far superior to typing raw git commands or using a bloated GUI. The tooling does the heavy lifting so I can focus on the output.

GNOME needs discipline to be useful out of the box. I use **Pop Shell** ([extensions.gnome.org](https://extensions.gnome.org/extension/4000/pop-shell/)) to enforce window tiling, **Vitals** to keep an eye on system metrics, and **Blur My Shell** for a cohesive aesthetic. Pop Shell's tiling logic is far more intuitive and integrated than setting up a standalone window manager like i3 or sway from scratch.

My GUI toolchain is a defensive architecture. It actively rejects the decay of modern computing—Electron-wrapped web apps and telemetry-harvesting SaaS subscriptions.

* **Web:** **Zen Browser** ([zen-browser.app](https://zen-browser.app/)). It destroys Chrome in privacy and outperforms standard Firefox by stripping out the Mozilla-mandated bloatware. It is the fastest, cleanest web engine available today.
* **Reading & Media:** **Calibre** ([calibre-ebook.com](https://calibre-ebook.com/)) holds my curated archive of over 4,200 ebooks. **Zotero** ([zotero.org](https://zotero.org/)) organizes academic references. Calibre runs circles around Apple Books or Kindle apps by giving you absolute, DRM-free control. Zotero crushes Mendeley by refusing to phone home. I also use **YACReader** ([yacreader.com](https://www.yacreader.com/)) which outperforms generic PDF readers with superior rendering for dense graphics, **Kasts** because it's lighter and faster than Spotify for podcasts, and **Nicotine+** ([nicotine-plus.org](https://nicotine-plus.org/)), the undisputed king of peer-to-peer music discovery that beats any algorithmic recommendation engine.
* **Comms:** **Halloy** ([github.com/squidowl/halloy](https://github.com/squidowl/halloy)) for IRC, **Fractal** for Matrix, **Tuba** for Mastodon. Unlike Discord or Slack, which are bloated Electron memory hogs, these are blazingly fast native applications that require zero browser tabs.
* **Finance:** **Actual** ([actualbudget.org](https://actualbudget.org/)) and **hledger** ([hledger.org](https://hledger.org/)) for budgeting. Mint and YNAB are consumer traps designed to harvest your financial data. Double-entry plaintext accounting forces mathematical discipline, and Actual provides a local-first UI that is fundamentally superior because you actually own the data, without the rent-seeking subscription.
* **Security:** **1Password**. While LastPass and Dashlane suffer constant breaches and atrocious UX, 1Password remains the gold standard, offering unmatched security architecture and native integration. I am militant about local-first software, but cryptography is hard. Rolling your own sync protocol for credentials is a fool's errand. You outsource this to audited professionals.

For RSS, I use an **Inoreader** backend because its filtering capabilities are miles ahead of Feedly. Right now, I'm reading feeds on NewsFlash, which provides a much cleaner, native reading experience than any web-based aggregator.

## VI. Custom Code: Building What I Need
{: #custom-code}

Eventually, you get tired of compromising and just build the tool yourself. General-purpose software inevitably degrades. By building native Linux GTK4 apps backed by SQLite in WAL mode, I guarantee performance that Electron apps can never match. No network latency, no web-view overhead.

* **Framework:** I wrote this because I wanted a document viewer that didn't drag like an anchor. It beats Evince and Okular because it opens immediately and gets strictly out of the way.
* **Viaduct:** My own NetNewsWire port for Linux. Better than Liferea or FeedReader because it perfectly translates macOS-level UX polish to the GNOME desktop.
* **Quarry & Lattice:** Heavy-duty archivism requires dedicated tools. Quarry mass-polishes metadata for Calibre faster than any Python script, and Lattice manages a local 6,000-track music library with extreme prejudice, leaving bloated players like Rhythmbox in the dust.
* **Atrium:** Task management is currently split—Org-mode for school, Todoist with Rin for household chores. But I am slowly eating both workflows with Atrium. It beats commercial tools because it's an Org-mode app wearing an OmniFocus and Things 3 disguise. I get the aesthetic calm of a modern GUI with the architectural permanence of a SQLite database that round-trips to plaintext.

## VII. The iPhone: Triage & Consumption
{: #iphone}

A phone is a consumption and triage device. If you are doing real work on a 6-inch glass rectangle, something has gone wrong in your life. The homescreen is arranged for speed, not scrolling. I dictate where the attention goes.

**Page One:**
Anchored at the top is a large **Fantastical** widget. It leaves Apple’s native Calendar and Google Calendar in the dust—it synthesizes schedules seamlessly and its natural language parsing is simply unmatched by anything else on the market. The dock is the core, holding a **Learning** folder (Blackboard, Reminders, PCalc, Soulver), Messages, Safari, and **Gemini**. I maintain subscriptions to both ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro, but Claude Pro's reasoning capabilities and code generation currently beat the rest, which is why it sits on my primary grid. I currently have free Gemini Pro due to being a student.

The rest of the grid:
* **Standalone Apps:** **Raindrop.io** beats built-in browser bookmarks with superior tagging and archiving. **Overcast** destroys Apple Podcasts because it actually respects audio engineering with Smart Speed and Voice Boost. **foobar2000** is still the most efficient local audio player ever made, and **Awful** remains the absolute best dedicated forum client, period.
* **Important Folder:** Tangerine, Wealthsimple, Passwords, 1Password, Outlook, Anylist, Authy, LocalSend, Cronometer.
* **Feeds Folder:** Reddit, WhatsApp, Mail, **Ice Cubes** (leaps and bounds ahead of the official Mastodon app or Ivory in terms of UX customization), **HEY** email (filters out the noise better than Gmail ever could), and **NetNewsWire** (remains the fastest, most rock-solid RSS reader for Apple ecosystems).
* **Social Folder:** Discord, LinkedIn, Steam, Spotify, Snapchat, Phone, LibraryThing, GitHub.

**Page Two:**
Pure utility. A large **Todoist** widget showing Today's tasks ensures shared execution doesn't slip through the cracks; Todoist beats Apple Reminders and Microsoft To Do with its universal availability and superior natural language entry. A smart rotating widget underneath it takes up the rest of the screen (CARROT Weather, Mail, Gmail). **CARROT Weather** is there because it provides hyper-local forecasts and radar that obliterate the default iOS Weather app.
