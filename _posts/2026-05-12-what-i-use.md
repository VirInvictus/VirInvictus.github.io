In a kitchen, your knife roll is your livelihood. In a mining camp, bad gear gets you killed, or at the very least, fired. Software is safer, but the principle holds. Tools are leverage. If your environment is constantly fighting you, you are bleeding time and focus. 

Here is what I use to read, write, and build. No cloud dependencies, no marketing bullshit. Just local-first tools that do their job and get out of the way.

## I. The Iron & The OS
{: #the-iron}

I run a **ThinkPad T14s AMD Gen 6**. It is a workhorse, not a lifestyle accessory. The operating system is **Fedora 44 Workstation** under GNOME and Wayland. I keep Windows 11 Pro on a dual-boot because reality occasionally demands it, but the actual work happens in Linux. 

My phone is an **iPhone 16 Pro**. I value good typography, polished hardware, and an opinionated ecosystem when I'm away from the keyboard, even if I strictly reject that locked-down philosophy at my desk.

## II. Aesthetics as Ergonomics
{: #aesthetics}

Visuals aren't vanity; they dictate how long you can stare at a screen before your eyes bleed. I favor a minimalist, industrial look. The terminal runs **Gruber Dark**. Everything else—from Neovim to standalone GUI tools—is bathed in **Kanagawa Dragon**. 

I do not tolerate system default fonts. My terminal and code are rendered in **TX-02** (semi-light, 18pt). Prose in Emacs gets **Tiempos Text**. These are premium, licensed fonts. Good typography is a cheap luxury that pays compounding dividends on your attention span. 

## III. The Two-Brain Architecture
{: #two-brain}

Trying to make one piece of software do everything is a fool's errand. It guarantees mediocrity. I enforce a strict separation of concerns between writing code and running my life. Doom Emacs is the brain; LazyVim is the scalpel.

* **The Brain (Doom Emacs):** This is the operational backend for my life. It is heavy and deeply customized. It handles my GTD workflow, spaced repetition, journaling, course management, and financial tracking in plaintext Org-tables. `config.org` is the single source of truth. All changes go through it. 
* **The Scalpel (LazyVim):** This is the code editor. Transparent background, ruthless minimalism, and a lean plugin surface. It relies on Mason-managed LSPs (`rust-analyzer`, `pyright`, `jdtls`) to do the heavy lifting. I don't live here; I just cut code here and leave.

## IV. The Terminal Ecosystem
{: #the-terminal}

Nostalgia for 1980s POSIX defaults is a disease. I respect the history of `ls` and `grep`, but I don't use them. My shell environment is built entirely on modern, high-performance replacements, mostly written in Rust or Go.

**Ghostty** is the terminal emulator, running **zsh** multiplexed through **tmux**. 

I use `eza` instead of `ls`, `bat` instead of `cat`, `rg` instead of `grep`, and `fd` instead of `find`. Directory jumping is handled by `zoxide`. `lazygit` handles version control, with `delta` wired in as the git pager. It's fast, colorful, and gives me exactly the context I need without archaic flags. 

## V. The Toolchain & The Non-Negotiable
{: #the-toolchain}

I write in C, C++, Rust, and Python, with a soft spot for Ruby. I manage runtimes cleanly via `uv`, `rustup`, and `rbenv`. 

My hard rule for software: **Local-first. Always.**

I don't reach for Docker. I can't think of many situations where a containerized operating system is the right answer for native desktop software. I build native GTK4 apps backed by SQLite on the user's disk in WAL mode. Syncing is secondary to a working local product. If the internet goes down, my environment doesn't even blink.
